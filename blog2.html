Analysis of Apple: Public Safety VS. Privacy

April 18, 2022

Background Established in 1976, Apple started as a manufacturer of personal computers. By 2021, Apple's products had a wide range of electronic devices, software, and online services. iPhone is Apple's most profitable product, and it accounted for 95% of the company's net sales in 2014. In 2015, Apple manufactured 15% of smartphones shipped worldwide but accounted for 95% of industry profits (McGee et al., 2021). Apple's headquarters is located in Cupertino, California, but most of the hardware of Apple's products is manufactured in China. Apple values the importance of clients' needs for privacy and security, and the company tries to prevent anyone, including the government, from having free access to Apple client's data.

Google is one of Apple's main competitors and it is also a California-based Internet company, and its products include a web browser, email service, cloud service, and mobile payment system. Google launched the Android OS system to compete with Apple's iOS system in 2008 and dominated the market: in 2014, 81% of total global smartphone shipments and 52% of tablet shipments ran on Android (McGee et al., 2021). Besides smartphone OS, Apple competes fiercely with Google and others in the technology industry. Since these tech companies store a vast amount of user data, there are many privacy and security issues.

In June 2013, Edward Snowden exposed an NSA program called PRISM, which allowed intelligence agencies to collect data from Internet companies such as Google and Facebook using a variety of technical means (McGee et al., 2021). The revelation impacted the tech industry heavily, and many major firms saw a drop in customers. As a result, American tech companies decided to protect their reputation and improve their privacy policies. Major tech companies, including Facebook, Google, and Apple, started to invest in measurements to enhance the privacy and security of their products and services.

User privacy is considered a top priority at Apple. In 2016 a federal judge ordered Apple to provide technical assistance to unlock the iPhone used by a murderer who, along with his wife, killed 14 people in San Bernardino, California. Apple refused to comply with the order because it violated Apple's policy of protecting users' privacy. On the one hand, many national laws guarantee people a level of privacy (see Exhibit 0). On the other hand, regulators must update the outdated surveillance laws for criminal activity according to the digital age. Privacy would come at a cost to allow law enforcement to combat crime.
