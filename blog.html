Digitization Disruption: Strategic Advantage for Growth and Surviving

April 4, 2022

Background Adidas started as a footwear-only company, but the entry of new competitors such as Nike and Reebok shook the market. After modifying its strategy, Adidas became marketing-driven instead of sales-driven; this helped focus on its weak points and achieve growth. In addition, Adidas made decisions such as increasing the range of products and acquiring other companies; by 2020, its products were sold in roughly 160 countries (Pisani & Lupoi, 2020). By the end of 2019, Adidas was the leading sportswear manufacturer in Europe and was second to Nike in the global market. Its two brands: Adidas and Reebok, valued at $11.2 billion and $800 million respectively, made it one of the most valuable sportswear companies (Pisani & Lupoi, 2020).

Adidas had one crucial disadvantage, its supply chain, Adidas' production was mainly outsourced to Asia (see Exhibit 1). This geographic restriction and inability to accurately predict demand and meet customers' needs for personalization impacted Adidas' sales in 2018 and 2019. In addition to this, digitalization disrupted retail and changed consumer behavior; retail had different types of customers (see Exhibit 2).

Adidas' response to these challenges was to make its manufacturing operations faster, acquire information from users by acquiring sporting apps, and launch its app to collect data on users and better attend the e-commerce channel. Such focus on digitalization proved convenient when COVID-19 hit (e-commerce channel had a growth of 35% during the first quarter (Pisani & Lupoi, 2020)). However, after surviving the crisis, the question remains if Adidas can maintain its status in an ever-changing digital environment.
